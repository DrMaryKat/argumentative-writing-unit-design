The STEP Framework for Ethical Reasoning

üéØ What is STEP? A systematic way to think through complex ethical issues without getting overwhelmed or oversimplifying.
S
Stakeholders

Who is affected by this decision or policy? Think beyond just "two sides" - who else has a stake in the outcome?

Ask yourself:

  • Who benefits? Who is harmed?
  • Who has power to decide?
  • Who has no voice in this decision?
  • What about future generations?

Remember: Look for indirect effects and "invisible" stakeholders

T
Trade-offs

What values, benefits, or priorities compete with each other? What do we gain and lose with different choices?

Ask yourself:

  • What competing values are at stake?
  • What are short vs. long-term costs?
  • Who gains vs. who loses?
  • What happens if we do nothing?

Common tensions: Freedom vs. Security, Innovation vs. Caution

E
Ethical Principles

What moral guidelines apply? Think about fairness, autonomy, responsibility, and doing good vs. avoiding harm.

Ask yourself:

  • What would be fair to everyone?
  • Does this respect people's autonomy?
  • Who is responsible for outcomes?
  • Does this maximize good or minimize harm?

Key principles: Justice, Autonomy, Beneficence, Non-maleficence

P
Perspectives

How do different cultural, generational, or social viewpoints understand this issue differently?

Ask yourself:

  • How might age affect views on this?
  • What about different cultural backgrounds?
  • How do economic circumstances matter?
  • What experiences shape different views?

Consider: Generational, cultural, economic, and experiential differences

STEP in Action: Example

Issue: Should colleges require attendance?

  • Stakeholders: Students (traditional & non-traditional), faculty, employers, families paying tuition
  • Trade-offs: Flexibility vs. accountability, individual choice vs. institutional standards
  • Ethical Principles: Fairness (equal treatment), autonomy (personal choice), responsibility
  • Perspectives: Different cultural attitudes toward authority, varying life circumstances

From STEP Analysis to Thesis Development

How STEP components work together:

  1. Start with Stakeholders: Who is most affected by your position?
  2. Identify key Trade-offs: What competing values does your position prioritize?
  3. Apply Ethical Principles: What moral framework justifies your choice?
  4. Consider Perspectives: How do different viewpoints challenge or support your position?

Result: A nuanced thesis that acknowledges complexity while taking a clear position based on systematic ethical reasoning.

Common STEP Mistakes to Avoid

  • Stakeholders: Only considering obvious groups (missing vulnerable or future stakeholders)
  • Trade-offs: Treating it as simple "pros vs. cons" (missing underlying value conflicts)
  • Ethical Principles: Applying only one principle (missing the complexity of competing moral claims)
  • Perspectives: Stereotyping groups (assuming all people in a category think the same way)
